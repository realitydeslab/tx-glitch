# Ethnographic Methods for Technology

Literature review for ARIA Scaling Trust application — Trust.Fail project.

## Key Papers

### Foundational Digital Ethnography

1. **Hine, C. (2000). *Virtual Ethnography*. SAGE Publications.**
   - URL: https://uk.sagepub.com/en-gb/eur/virtual-ethnography/book210958
   - Foundational text establishing methods for conducting ethnography in online spaces. Argues internet is both culture and cultural artefact.
   - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail's Discord-based interview approach is a form of virtual ethnography — collecting accounts of human-AI interaction through digital platforms.

2. **Pink, S., Horst, H., Postill, J., Hjorth, L., Lewis, T., & Tacchi, J. (2016). *Digital Ethnography: Principles and Practice*. SAGE Publications.**
   - URL: https://uk.sagepub.com/en-gb/eur/digital-ethnography/book243111
   - Comprehensive methodology for ethnographic research in digital environments. Emphasises sensory, experiential, and material dimensions.
   - **Relevance:** Provides methodological grounding for Trust.Fail's multi-surface data collection (Discord, voice, web).

3. **Boellstorff, T., Nardi, B., Pearce, C., & Taylor, T.L. (2012). *Ethnography and Virtual Worlds: A Handbook of Method*. Princeton University Press.**
   - URL: https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691149516/ethnography-and-virtual-worlds
   - Practical handbook for conducting ethnographic research in virtual environments. Covers participant observation, interviews, and ethics.
   - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail operates in hybrid digital-physical spaces; this handbook's methods inform our multi-surface interview design.

### Ethnography in HCI

4. **Dourish, P. (2006). Implications for Design. *Proceedings of the SIGCHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems (CHI '06)*, 541–550.**
   - DOI: 10.1145/1124772.1124855
   - Critiques the "implications for design" framing in HCI ethnography. Argues ethnographic work has value as knowledge production, not just design input.
   - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail produces empirical knowledge about trust failures that has intrinsic value beyond immediate design implications — supporting Track 3.4 Bluesky framing.

5. **Crabtree, A., Rouncefield, M., & Tolmie, P. (2012). *Doing Design Ethnography*. Springer.**
   - DOI: 10.1007/978-1-4471-2726-0
   - Practical guide to ethnographic practice in systems design. Covers fieldwork, analysis, and the relationship between ethnography and design.
   - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail's methodology bridges ethnographic observation and systems design — producing structured data that informs security tool design.

6. **Randall, D., Harper, R., & Rouncefield, M. (2007). *Fieldwork for Design: Theory and Practice*. Springer.**
   - DOI: 10.1007/978-1-84628-768-8
   - Comprehensive account of ethnographic fieldwork methods for technology design. Covers workplace studies, domestic settings, and public spaces.
   - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail extends this tradition to human-AI interaction — fieldwork for security design.

### Auto-ethnography & Reflexive Methods

7. **Ellis, C., Adams, T.E., & Bochner, A.P. (2011). Autoethnography: An Overview. *Forum: Qualitative Social Research*, 12(1).**
   - DOI: 10.17169/fqs-12.1.1589
   - Defines autoethnography as a research method that connects personal experience to wider cultural, political, and social meanings.
   - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail interviews are essentially solicited autoethnographies — participants narrate their personal trust failure experiences.

8. **Chang, H. (2008). *Autoethnography as Method*. Routledge.**
   - DOI: 10.4324/9781315433370
   - Systematic guide to autoethnographic research. Covers data collection, analysis, and ethical considerations.
   - **Relevance:** Our interview protocol draws on autoethnographic principles — structured self-reflection on technology encounters.

### Experience Sampling & Diary Methods

9. **Consolvo, S. & Walker, M. (2003). Using the Experience Sampling Method to Evaluate Ubicomp Applications. *IEEE Pervasive Computing*, 2(2), 24–31.**
   - DOI: 10.1109/MPRV.2003.1203750
   - Applies experience sampling method (ESM) to evaluate ubiquitous computing. Shows how in-situ reporting captures experiences that retrospective methods miss.
   - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail's "report when it happens" approach is inspired by ESM — capturing trust glitches close to the moment of occurrence.

10. **Bolger, N., Davis, A., & Rafaeli, E. (2003). Diary Methods: Capturing Life as it is Lived. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 54, 579–616.**
    - DOI: 10.1146/annurev.psych.54.101601.145030
    - Reviews diary methods for capturing daily experiences. Covers design, analysis, and validity considerations.
    - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail's ethnographic cards function as structured diary entries of trust encounters — similar to diary study methodology.

### Critical & Feminist Approaches

11. **Suchman, L. (2007). *Human-Machine Reconfigurations: Plans and Situated Actions* (2nd ed.). Cambridge University Press.**
    - DOI: 10.1017/CBO9780511808418
    - Foundational critique of AI's assumptions about human-machine interaction. Argues for situated, ethnographic understanding over abstract models.
    - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail is deeply aligned with Suchman's approach — understanding trust through situated accounts rather than abstract trust models.

12. **Barad, K. (2007). *Meeting the Universe Halfway: Quantum Physics and the Entanglement of Matter and Meaning*. Duke University Press.**
    - DOI: 10.1215/9780822388128
    - Proposes "agential realism" — matter and meaning are entangled; boundaries between human and nonhuman are performatively enacted.
    - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail's ethological framing treats AI agents as entities whose behaviour can be studied empirically, aligning with posthumanist methodologies.

### Qualitative Methods in AI/ML Research

13. **Chancellor, S. & Baumer, E.P.S. (2022). Human-Centered Algorithm Design: Challenges and Opportunities. *Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction*, 6(CSCW2), Article 461.**
    - DOI: 10.1145/3555578
    - Reviews qualitative approaches to understanding algorithmic systems. Argues for human-centered perspectives on algorithm design.
    - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail extends this to trust — using qualitative methods to understand how algorithmic behaviour creates trust failures.

14. **Seaver, N. (2017). Algorithms as culture: Some tactics for the ethnography of algorithmic systems. *Big Data & Society*, 4(2).**
    - DOI: 10.1177/2053951717738104
    - Proposes ethnographic tactics for studying algorithms as cultural systems. Includes "scavenging" — piecing together accounts from users, developers, and observers.
    - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail's interview methodology is a systematic form of Seaver's "scavenging" — collecting diverse accounts of algorithmic trust failures.

15. **Burrell, J. (2016). How the machine 'thinks': Understanding opacity in machine learning algorithms. *Big Data & Society*, 3(1).**
    - DOI: 10.1177/2053951715622512
    - Identifies three forms of opacity in ML systems: intentional secrecy, technical illiteracy, and intrinsic complexity. Shows why ethnographic approaches are needed.
    - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail documents how opacity contributes to trust failures — participants often cannot explain *why* the AI behaved unexpectedly.

### Technology-Specific Ethnographies

16. **Lupton, D. (2020). *Data Selves: More-than-Human Perspectives*. Polity Press.**
    - URL: https://politybooks.com/bookdetail?book_slug=data-selves-more-than-human-perspectives--9781509536429
    - Examines how humans relate to their digital data through more-than-human and posthumanist frameworks.
    - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail studies the relationship between humans and AI agents — a more-than-human interaction where trust is a relational property.

17. **Vertesi, J. (2015). *Seeing Like a Rover: How Robots, Teams, and Images Craft Knowledge of Mars*. University of Chicago Press.**
    - DOI: 10.7208/chicago/9780226156019.001.0001
    - Ethnography of NASA's Mars rover teams. Shows how human-robot collaboration produces knowledge through negotiated trust.
    - **Relevance:** Demonstrates that trust in autonomous systems is socially constructed — directly relevant to Trust.Fail's approach of studying trust as an interactional phenomenon.

18. **Elish, M.C. (2019). Moral Crumple Zones: Cautionary Tales in Human-Robot Interaction. *Engaging Science, Technology, and Society*, 5, 40–60.**
    - DOI: 10.17351/ests2019.260
    - Introduces "moral crumple zone" concept — humans absorb blame for AI system failures. Ethnographic study of autonomous vehicle and surgical robot incidents.
    - **Relevance:** Trust.Fail documents exactly these moral crumple zone moments — when AI fails and humans bear the consequences.

## Summary

This literature establishes strong methodological foundations for Trust.Fail:
- **Digital ethnography** methods validate our multi-surface, online interview approach
- **Experience sampling and diary methods** support our "capture when it happens" design
- **Autoethnographic principles** inform our participant-as-narrator interview structure
- **STS and posthumanist frameworks** ground our ethological treatment of AI agents as entities worthy of behavioural study
- **Gap:** No existing work systematically applies ethnographic methods to collect and code AI trust failures at scale using an ethological framework — this is Trust.Fail's unique contribution.
